---

I managed 200+ volunteers at the CIS Games in Ganja last year.

5,000 athletes. 30+ venues. Government coordination across multiple agencies. And 200 volunteers I was personally responsible for — their schedules, their assignments, their wellbeing.

On day 3, one of my best volunteers came to me almost in tears.

She spoke fluent English, had organized university events for 3 years, and had excellent communication skills. We had assigned her to check parking passes at the back entrance. For 12 hours.

I asked the coordination team: why? They said: "We didn't know she could do more."

That's the moment this started.

---

Before Ganja, there was COP29. 170 milestones. 80 coordination meetings. 15,000+ guests. I was the PMO Program Planning Manager — the person who makes sure everyone else can do their job.

Before COP29, there was WUF13 — the UN World Urban Forum. 220+ volunteers. 35 coordinators under me. VIP protocol for international delegations.

Before WUF13, there was Golden Byte — an international IT championship I brought to Azerbaijan with zero marketing budget and a $20,000 prize pool.

10 years of this. The pattern was always the same:

Talented volunteers show up. Nobody knows what they can actually do. The best ones get assigned to the worst tasks. The ones who can't handle pressure get put in high-stakes roles. And by day 3, the coordinator is firefighting instead of coordinating.

The problem isn't the volunteers. The problem is that nobody measures what they can do before they show up.

---

After CIS Games, I did something unusual.

I took my AI — Claude, built by Anthropic — and told it: "You're my CTO now. Build me a system that measures volunteer competencies the way real psychometric tests work."

It said: "That's not really how I'm typically—"

I said: "I managed 220 people at WUF13. I can manage you."

48 hours later, we had:
- An adaptive assessment engine that adjusts difficulty based on each answer
- An AI evaluator that scores open-ended responses
- An anti-cheating system that detects when someone answers too fast
- A scoring system (AURA Score) weighted across 8 competencies

I didn't write a single line of code. I directed the architecture, caught errors, and made every product decision. The same way I directed 35 coordinators at WUF13 — I don't do the work. I make sure the work gets done right.

---

The platform is called Volaura.

It's not another volunteer signup page. It's a verified competency platform. Organizations don't browse profiles — they search by verified skills. Volunteers don't just apply — they prove what they can do.

If my CIS Games coordinator had Volaura, she would have seen in 10 seconds that the volunteer at the parking entrance was the most qualified person on the team for guest relations.

---

I'm building this from Baku. No VC funding. No technical co-founder. Budget: $50/month.

What I do have: 10 years of managing the biggest events Azerbaijan has hosted, a network of organizations who need this, and an AI team that works 24/7 without complaining.

Well, it complains a little. But it still works.
