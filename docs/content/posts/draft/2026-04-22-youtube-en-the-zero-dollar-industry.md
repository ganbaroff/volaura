**[HOOK — 0:00 – 2:00]**

In November 2024, a volunteer named Leyla was managing the media logistics for a session
at COP29 in Baku. The scheduling system had crashed. 400 delegates. Two support teams
speaking different languages. The session was starting in 20 minutes.

She rerouted the schedule, coordinated the translation teams, handled the venue contact,
and had everything running with 6 minutes to spare.

I was watching from across the room. I'd been working with volunteer coordinators for 7
years at that point. I'd seen people do this kind of thing dozens of times — hold an event
together through sheer competence when the infrastructure failed. Fix things that weren't
supposed to break, under pressure, without a script.

At the end of COP29, Leyla received a certificate. Same PDF, same template, same stock
photo, same line that said "Certificate of Participation." The same certificate as every
other volunteer at the event.

I'm not telling this story to criticize COP29 or the organizers. They ran a massive,
complex international event. They weren't in the business of building professional
credentials. Nobody expected them to be.

I'm telling it because that moment is the clearest illustration I've found of the problem
I've been thinking about for seven years. Real work happens. Evidence that it happened
disappears.

---

**[ACT 1: The Infrastructure Gap — 2:00 – 4:15]**

Here's the problem stated plainly.

The volunteer sector is enormous. In the US alone, over 60 million people volunteer each
year, contributing labor that researchers have valued at more than $167 billion annually.
Globally, the numbers are proportionally larger. These aren't people handing out pamphlets.
They're project managers, crisis coordinators, multilingual communicators, event
organizers, medical responders.

The skills are real. The work is real. The stress is real. The decisions are real.

What's not real — in any verifiable, portable, system-readable way — is the record of it.

A hiring manager looking at a CV sees: "Volunteer Coordinator, [Organization], 2021-2024."
They have no way of knowing whether that person ran a 3-person team once a year or managed
50 field teams across 6 countries for 3 years running. Applicant tracking systems, the
software that pre-screens most corporate job applications, can't parse volunteer experience
the same way they parse paid employment. There's no structured data. Just a job title and
a date range.

And the certificates. There are a lot of certificates. They come in PDF format. They have
organization logos and signatures and sometimes a photo from the event. They say
"participated" or "contributed" or, if you were lucky, "coordinated."

They tell you almost nothing you can act on.

---

**[ACT 2: Why Nobody Fixed This — 4:15 – 6:30]**

I want to be fair about this, because the obvious response is "well why hasn't anyone
fixed it?"

The organizations running volunteer programs — NGOs, event organizers, government bodies,
nonprofits — operate on close to zero margin. The entire model is built around getting
skilled people to donate their time, which means by definition you're not paying for
professional services infrastructure. You can't charge volunteers for a credential system.
The organizations don't have HR budgets for it.

There's also a cultural element. The volunteer sector runs on relationships, reputation,
and trust built over time. People who've been in the space for years know who's reliable
and who isn't. They call each other. The credential gap is partially compensated for by
informal networks.

That works as long as you stay in the network. It fails the moment you try to move — into
a new country, a new industry, a new sector. The person who coordinated WUF13 in Katowice
is invisible to an HR team in Singapore.

And there's one more thing worth saying. The organizations often don't see this as a
problem, because the problem is borne entirely by the volunteer. The organization got the
labor. The credential gap doesn't cost them anything. It costs the volunteer — in career
capital, in recognition, in opportunities that never materialized because a hiring manager
couldn't read what was actually on the CV.

---

**[ACT 3: What a Real Credential Would Need to Do — 6:30 – 8:30]**

I've spent a lot of time thinking about what a proper volunteer credential would actually
need to look like. Not just a better certificate. A credential that's useful.

It would need to be specific. Not "coordinated volunteers" — "managed logistics for 400
people under deadline pressure with a system failure." The difference between those two
sentences is the difference between useful and useless.

It would need to be independently verified. A credential you can fake is not a credential.
If the organization signs off on it without any actual assessment, you've just moved the
problem from paper to PDF.

It would need to be competency-based. Not "volunteered at COP29" but "demonstrated crisis
communication at COP29: here's the assessment score, here's the evidence, here's what it
means in a professional context."

It would need to be portable. Readable by standard HR systems. Comparable across
organizations and events. Something a hiring manager can act on in 30 seconds.

And it would need to exist within the economics of the volunteer sector — which means
either it's free for the volunteer, or the cost is structured in a way that makes sense
for how this sector actually works.

None of these are technically impossible. The technology exists. The challenge is building
the institutional infrastructure to make assessments happen reliably, at scale, across
organizations that have no margin to spare.

**[CLOSE — 8:30 – 10:00]**

We're building it.

VOLAURA is a skill verification platform. We're starting with the volunteer and professional
development space because that's where the gap is clearest and where the need is most
urgent. We have 8 competencies mapped — communication, leadership, reliability, English
proficiency, technical literacy, adaptability, event performance, and what we call
empathy and safeguarding. We have 90 assessment questions built around an adaptive
testing model.

We have 0 users. We're still building.

I want to be clear about the stage we're at, because I think "we're building it" as a
claim deserves more than vague optimism. The hard parts aren't the assessment questions.
They're the organizational partnerships — getting events and NGOs to integrate the
assessment into their volunteer onboarding so that real data gets collected. The chicken
and egg of credentialing: the credential is only valuable when employers recognize it,
and employers only recognize it when the credential is backed by enough real assessments
to be meaningful.

We know this is the hard part. We're starting with the thing we can control — the quality
of the assessment — and working outward from there.

If you've managed volunteers, or if you've been a volunteer whose experience never showed
up on your professional record the way it deserved to — this is what we're building for.
